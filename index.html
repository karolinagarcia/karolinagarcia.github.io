Academic Projects

    Here I explain the main projects I have led or been strongly involved in. For a full list of papers I collaborated on, please check Google Scholar.
  • Modeling intensity mapping experiments in cosmological hydrodynamical simulations
    K Garcia, D Narayanan, G Popping, R Anirudh, S Sutherland, M Kaasinen
    I led the development of a tool (slick) that combines cosmological hydrodynamic simulations with thermal/radiative/chemical equilibrium PDR models to calculate CO/[CI]/[CII] line luminosities from every gas particle in different redshifts of the simulation. With this tool, we are able to build light cones from z=0 to z=10. For more details, check out our paper on arXiv, submitted to ApJ.
  • New, fully symmetric, method for the 3PCF line of sight
    K Garcia, Z Slepian
    The 3-point correlation function (3PCF) is a powerful tool for cosmology as it goes beyond the 2PCF, and probes late-time non-Gaussianities. To access its information, we must track the 3PCF’s dependence not only on each triangle’s shape, but also on its orientation with respect to the line of sight. We developed a method that measures the 3PCF while keeping the line of sight fully symmetric between the triangles' triplet members. For more details, you can check out our paper published in MNRAS.
  • Finding galaxy clusters in the Boötes field with Spitzer
    K Garcia, A Gonzalez, M Brodwin, N Hansen, T George, A Trudeau, K Thongkham et al.
    We identified >4,000 galaxy cluster and group candidates in the 9.3 deg^2 Boötes region, among which >1,000 lie beyond z = 1.5, and >500 beyond z = 2. The catalog was built based on a wavelet-style algorithm and photometric redshift probability distributions using data from Spitzer Deep Wide-Field Survey, NOAO Deep Wide-Field Survey, Infrared Boötes Imaging Survey, and Large Binocular Cameras imaging.
  • Constraining σ8 with supernova peculiar velocities
    K Garcia, M Quartin, B B Siffert
    We investigated the optima survey strategies to measure peculiar velocity fields with Type Ia supernovae. By varying the observational parameters, such as the area of the sky we are looking at, the depth being reached and the cadence, we found out which combination gives the best estimate on σ8 using supernova peculiar velocities. For more details, take a look at the paper published in Physics of the Dark Universe.
  • Detection of Type Ia supernovae
    B B Siffert, K Garcia, M Quartin
    We developed a pipeline for supernovae detection focused on surveys that make use of narrow-band filters, such as J-PAS, J-PLUS and S-PLUS.
